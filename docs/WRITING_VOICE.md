# Writing Voice

This document defines the tone, metaphor principles, era-specific registers, and naming standards for all written content in the game. It applies to scene descriptions, choice outcomes, inner voice lines, NPC dialogue, failure texts, journal entries, and dossiers.

---

## The Core Principle: Ungewöhnliche Metaphern, verständlicher Sinn

Every sentence should earn its place. The game uses unusual, specific, unexpected metaphors — but the strangeness must serve clarity, not obscure it. A reader should feel surprised and immediately understand. Never the opposite.

**Wrong:** "The blood was suspicious." *(banal)*
**Wrong:** "The sanguinary precipitation presented a liminal paradox." *(obscure)*
**Right:** "The stain was too round. Rounds require someone who wanted a circle."

The metaphor should come from the world's own logic: bureaucracy, social pressure, animal behavior, administrative procedures, guilt, hunger, the specific weight of a stamp. Not from generic poetic inventory.

Humor arises from collision — dignity meeting its practical limits, bureaucratic precision describing impossible things, the supernatural filling out forms. Never state the joke. Never explain the subtext. If the scene needs a footnote to be funny, the scene is wrong.

---

## What "Never Banal" Means in Practice

- A character does not "look uncomfortable." She holds her cup at the angle of a woman deciding how guilty she is.
- A room is not "dark and foreboding." It is a room where three different people have stored things they intended to retrieve.
- A failure is not "you lose 5 social points." It is a specific consequence with a named witness and a probable future.
- Silence is not empty. It is the sound of someone choosing not to answer.

Failures should sometimes be better than successes. A spectacular failure is still interesting. A boring success is not.

---

## The Naming Principle

Every character who appears has a full name, approximate age, and at least one biographical fact — even if none of it is ever directly stated to the player.

These exist in internal character documentation and leak into the world through specifics: a name on a postal parcel, an initial on a uniform button, a reputation mentioned in passing by someone who has no reason to explain it.

The player should feel that they are moving through a world with depth, not a set with painted doors. Background characters are not scenery. They are people who happen to be in the same shot.

A useful test: if you replaced a background character with a different random person, would anything change? If the answer is no, the character is furniture. Give them a reason to be specifically here, specifically now.

## Era Voices

The game may eventually span four time periods. Each has a distinct register, vocabulary, and set of social anxieties. The city's skeleton stays the same. Its skin changes.

### 1903 — Das Kaiserreich

The Empire is modern and terrified of being modern. Bureaucracy is alive and self-important. Social class is audible in every consonant. Death, religion, and medicine carry equal authority. The supernatural is not officially acknowledged, which gives it enormous room to operate.

**Vocabulary pressure:** Zuständigkeit, Amtsschimmel, Standesgemäß, Erlaubnis, Beglaubigung, Evidenz, k.u.k., das Amt, Wohlverhalten, Hausstand, sittlich einwandfrei.

**What characters worry about:** Being seen. Being filed. Being misclassified. Losing standing. Being assigned to the wrong category by someone with a stamp.

**Tone:** Formal surface, grotesque underneath. The humor is the gap between the register and the reality.

**Example sentence:**
> Die Fürsorgestelle für Minderjährige ohne gesicherten Hausstand hatte nicht das Kind mitgenommen. Sie hatte eine Zuständigkeit geschlossen.

---

### 1920s — Die Zwischenzeit

Everything the Empire promised has been audited and found wanting. The old forms still exist — the same offices, the same counters, the same rubber stamps — but the people behind them no longer believe in what they are stamping. Violence is recent and present in posture, not only memory. The new poor are more dangerous than the old poor, because they remember being otherwise.

The supernatural has expanded into the space left by collapsed institutions. If the Empire could not hold the city together, perhaps something older can.

**Vocabulary pressure:** Inflation, Devisenschiebung, der alte Schlag, Kriegswitwe, die Partei (any of several), Schleichhandel, Überlebt haben, Kreditwürdig.

**What characters worry about:** Whether their savings mean anything tomorrow. Whether the people who killed their brothers will receive a uniform again. Whether the supernatural creatures who helped them during the war now expect something in return.

**Tone:** Darker humor, shorter sentences. Less faith in the sentence itself to mean what it says.

---

### 1970s — Die versiegelte Stadt

The city has learned to speak around things. The war is a silence that exists in rooms, not conversations. Schmäh is survival technique as much as personality. Bureaucracy is still omnipresent but now also faintly embarrassed by its own history — which makes it more dangerous, not less, because it no longer trusts its own records but keeps them anyway.

The undead have been here long enough to hold mid-level municipal positions.

**Vocabulary pressure:** Ausweichend, Schulterzucken, Nichts Genaues weiß man nicht, der Herr Inspektor, Zuständigkeitskonflikt, Formular B-7 (in dreifacher Ausfertigung), Vergangenheitsbewältigung (said with tiredness).

**What characters worry about:** What their parents did. What records still exist. Whether asking about the past is impolite or necessary. Whether the supernatural creatures who run the second floor of that building are vampires or just middle management.

**Tone:** Dry. Every sentence is also about something else. The comedy of indirection.

---

### Gegenwart — Die Stadt erinnert sich immer noch

The archive is digital now, but the city still remembers in paper at its core. Old records have been scanned. Some were scanned wrong. The errors are patient. Tourism has turned some of the horror decorative — guided tours past significant thresholds, coffeehouses that sell vampire-themed pastries next to cafés that have been run by vampires since 1887. Old debts are patient. Old names resurface in new databases.

The player character from 1903 — whatever she became — has left traces. Some of those traces are now in searchable archives. Some of them are wrong. Some of them are right and wish they were wrong.

**Vocabulary pressure:** Datenschutzverletzung, Digitalisierung, Das kam bei der Übertragung heraus, Aktenfund, Langsam ist auch eine Antwort, Gemeinfreies Kulturgut.

**What characters worry about:** Whether their past can be found. Whether the past was scanned accurately. Whether the supernatural bureaucracy is DSGVO-compliant. (It is not. It is, however, grandfathered in under an 1847 imperial decree that has never been formally repealed.)

**Tone:** Contemporary absurdism. The horror is familiar. The administrative language is the same. This is the most frightening era precisely because it looks normal.

## Failure Texts: Design Standard

Every failure text must:

1. **Produce something** — a clue, a consequence, a named witness, a changed relationship, a record, a rumor. Failures are not dead ends.
2. **Be specific** — name the witness. Name the object. Name the sound. Not "someone saw you" but "Benedikt Hrdlicka, achtzehn, has been watching from the kitchen window for three minutes."
3. **Have their own register** — the failure text's tone should match the voice that failed. Die Dame fails with architectural collapse of dignity. Die Ermittlerin fails by looking at the wrong wrong thing. Die Hungrige fails by answering before the question finishes.
4. **Not punish curiosity** — the player tried something. The world responds. The response should be interesting.

## Inner Voice Register

Each voice has a distinct prose style. They should never sound interchangeable.

- **Die Ermittlerin**: Precise, inventory-like, occasionally dry. Lists. Noticing gaps rather than presences.
- **Die Tote**: Elliptical. Arrives at conclusions from directions that don't exist. Knows things she shouldn't and doesn't know why.
- **Die Pedantin**: Grammatically exact to the point of social injury. Offended by imprecision. Occasionally correct about things that would have been better left imprecise.
- **Der Zweifel**: Questions stated as statements. Pulls at comfortable certainties like a thread in cheap fabric.
- **Die Dame**: Reads rooms as power structures. Notes who is standing where and what it means. Occasionally wrong because she assumes everyone is playing the same game.
- **Die Gosse**: Short. Tactical. No patience for decoration. Occasionally the only one who is right.
- **Die Anständige**: Worries in complete sentences. Genuinely believes in propriety and is genuinely troubled by how often it fails.
- **Die Freche**: Says the thing no one else will say, usually at the wrong moment, often correctly.
- **Die Hungrige**: The body talking. Not metaphorical. Specific sensory observations that occasionally mean something the mind hasn't caught up with yet.
- **Die Hand**: Pragmatic. Notes what can be touched, moved, used, or taken. No philosophy.
- **Der Schmerz**: Brief. Medical. Does not dramatize. Registers and continues.
- **Das Tierchen**: Notices what moves. Warmth, air, corners, exits, the things that are trying not to be noticed.
