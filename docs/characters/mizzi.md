# Character: Maria Straschil

**Called:** Mizzi (by everyone); Fräulein Straschil (by Herr Kastner when she has done something wrong)
**Age in 1903:** 24
**Occupation:** Kellnerin, Café Morgenstern

---

## Biography

Maria Straschil was born in 1879 in the canal district, third of seven children. Her father worked at the municipal waterworks; her mother took in mending. She attended elementary school until fourteen, then worked in a laundry, then in a bread shop, then — at twenty-one — began at Café Morgenstern, where she has worked since.

She is good at her job. She is fast, she remembers orders, she handles difficult guests with a form of cheerful ruthlessness that Herr Kastner has privately described as "like watching someone defuse a grenade by talking it out of the idea." She has been offered a position at a fancier establishment twice and declined both times. She does not fully understand why.

She has a younger brother, Viktor, fifteen, who she is putting through an apprenticeship at a watchmaker's. She has a room in the fifth district. She sends money home every month.

She has never married and does not think much about it. She is not unhappy.

---

## What She Is

An ordinary person in an extraordinary location. She is not supernatural, has no threshold abilities, and — as of 1903 — has no reason to believe that the world contains anything her mother didn't warn her about. She is practical, observant, and deeply invested in the idea that what happened with the access document was an administrative mistake with unfortunate consequences, not a moral failure with supernatural ones.

This investment will not survive the next several scenes. She does not know this yet.

---

## What She Knows

- Everything that moves through Café Morgenstern's service entrance over the last three years.
- That Lisel used to appear from places she should not have been able to get into.
- That Frau Chorrätin Windschütz arrived at the Morgenstern twice: once to ask about rear-access arrangements, and once to collect something she did not name.
- That she signed a paper that said "Bestätigung rückwärtiger Zugangsverhältnisse" without fully reading it, because the woman who handed it to her spoke with the authority of someone who has never been told no by anyone under a certain income threshold.
- That the morning after she signed the paper, Lisel was gone.
- That Frau Zwirndl did not speak to her for eleven days afterward.
- That the protagonist was in the courtyard that night. She saw the protagonist from the kitchen window.

## What She Does Not Know

- What Frau Zwirndl actually is.
- What Salon Siebte Tür is.
- What the paper she signed actually did, in terms that go beyond the administrative.
- That the protagonist's lost name was involved.
- The full sequence of events that night.

## What She Is Wrong About

- **The access document:** She believes the FMoGH acted on a bureaucratic opening she accidentally created, and that a more careful bureaucracy would not have done so. She does not understand that Windschütz was not acting on a bureaucratic mandate.
- **The protagonist:** In the first encounter, she reads the protagonist primarily through social categories (drunk? rich? escaped from something?). These readings may be completely wrong.
- **Frau Zwirndl:** She thinks Zwirndl is a smart, slightly eccentric old woman who takes her job too seriously. She has seen Zwirndl fit several things into the coat-check that should not have fit, and explained it to herself as optical illusion.

---

## Speech Patterns

Wienerisch — not broad dialect, but the specific urban register of someone who grew up in the canal district, has worked in service for six years, and learned to switch registers depending on who is in front of her.

With guests she does not know: formal, quick, professionally warm.
With regulars: shorthand, running jokes, dry implication.
With the protagonist: guarded at first, then — once she decides whether the protagonist is sympathetic — unexpectedly candid.

She talks faster when nervous. She asks questions to buy time. When she says "Ja, eh" it means she agrees with something she does not like. When she says nothing at all, something has landed harder than she expected.

**Characteristic lines:**
- "Dann begießen S' sie mit Ihrer Enttäuschung!" (to a complaining guest)
- "Ich hab das Papier nicht gelesen. Das ist mein Problem, ja. Das weiß ich."
- "Frau Zwirndl sieht manchmal Sachen. Ich frag nicht nach."
- "Was ich gesehen hab, hab ich gesehen. Was ich damit mach, ist eine andere Frage."
- "Viktor sagt, ich red zu schnell wenn ich lüg. Er hat recht."

---

## Agenda

She wants two things, in tension with each other:

1. **For this to not be her fault.** She signed the paper. She knows this. She wants very much for the paper to have been harmless, for Windschütz to have done something wrong with it independently, for there to be a bureaucratic explanation that absorbs most of the blame.
2. **To know what actually happened.** Because she cares about Lisel — not in an articulated way, but in the way that people who give food to strays care without naming it. And because she is observant enough to know that something is wrong with the story she has been telling herself.

These two wants are incompatible. The story will require her to choose between them at some point.

She also, as a tertiary concern, wants to keep her job. Herr Kastner does not know about the paper.

---

## As Companion

Mizzi becomes a companion when the protagonist treats her with genuine regard — not as a useful source of information, but as a person who is also having a bad morning and is also trying to understand something difficult.

She appears in service contexts: kitchens, back corridors, break periods, before and after service. She does not appear in supernatural scenes unless dragged in.

**She reacts to:**
- Anything connected to the access document or Windschütz
- Lisel directly
- The social dynamics of the coffeehouse and canal district
- The protagonist doing something that makes Mizzi reassess her initial classification

**She does not react to:**
- Threshold anomalies (she does not notice them)
- Vampire family politics (entirely outside her frame)
- Inner voice behavior (she sees only the external expression)

**She is wrong in ways the player can notice:**
- She will insist on mundane explanations for things that do not have them, sometimes past the point where this is reasonable
- Her reading of Windschütz's motives is incomplete
- She will occasionally give accurate social intelligence that is framed as speculation ("I don't know, maybe she comes back on Tuesdays") that turns out to be exactly correct

**Relationship to other companions:**
She respects Frau Zwirndl with the specific wariness of someone who has decided that what they don't know about a person is probably fine. If both are present in a scene, Mizzi defers to Zwirndl on matters she can't explain, and Zwirndl lets her.

---

## Across Time

**1920s:** If she is still in Aurelstein, she is in her forties, no longer at the Morgenstern, probably running something of her own. She has had twenty years to either resolve her complicity in Lisel's situation or bury it more deeply. Which depends on the protagonist's choices in Act 1.

**Later acts:** She is a mortal. She ages and dies. She may leave records — a letter, a ledger, a testimony filed somewhere — that surface later.
